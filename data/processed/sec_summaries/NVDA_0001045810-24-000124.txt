• **Supply Chain Aggression:** Total supply commitments surged to $34.1B ($18.8B purchase obligations + $15.3B long-term prepayments). This 16% sequential increase in purchase obligations signals an aggressive attempt to monopolize CoWoS and HBM3e capacity to box out competitors ahead of the Blackwell ramp.
• **Extreme Customer Concentration:** Revenue concentration reached a critical pivot; two indirect customers now account for 24% and 15% of total revenue respectively ($8.8B total). This 39% concentration risk in the top two nodes suggests NVDA is increasingly a levered play on the CapEx budgets of just two hyperscalers.
• **Margin-Sentiment Divergence:** Despite management’s "infinite demand" narrative for Blackwell, Q2 non-GAAP Gross Margin guidance of 75.5% represents a 290bps sequential compression from Q1's 78.4%. This indicates "New Product Introduction" (NPI) costs and a less favorable mix are outweighing pricing power.
• **RPO Growth Trajectory:** Remaining Performance Obligations (RPO) hit $13.6B. Crucially, 40% ($5.4B) is now termed beyond 12 months, up from historical norms, signaling a shift toward multi-year sovereign AI and enterprise software contracts rather than just "spot" hardware sales.
• **Inventory Quality:** Inventory increased to $5.9B (up 11% QoQ), but finished goods remained flat at $4.0B. The growth is entirely in "Work-in-Process," confirming a massive Blackwell build-out rather than an accumulation of unsold Hopper units.
• **Sovereign AI Pivot:** Management explicitly modified risk factors to include "Sovereign AI" as a distinct vertical. This shifts the KPI focus from CSP CapEx to national industrial policy, providing a hedge against a potential US cloud spending slowdown.
• **Buyback Acceleration:** NVDA repurchased $9.5B in Q1 (including $1.3B in tax-related shares), utilizing 42% of its remaining $22.5B authorization in just three months—the most aggressive pace in the company's history.
• **China Decay:** Revenue from China (including HK) plummeted to a mid-single-digit percentage of Data Center revenue, down from 20–25% historically. This suggests US export controls have effectively neutralized China as a growth engine, making the 427% YoY Data Center growth even more impressive as it was achieved almost entirely in Western markets.
